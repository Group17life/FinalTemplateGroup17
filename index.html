About

While many people are aware of racism, it is mostly seen and acknowledged on the individual level. However, much of racism is continued or caused by institutional structures that have much larger effects that are often dismissed as just part of life. We aim to explore how race impacts overall health, education, employment, and housing status. All of these factors are part of an individual’s socioeconomic status and impact their everyday lives. Each of our articles will highlight different aspects of why this subject is important to understand. A better understanding of the correlation between race and socioeconomic status will inspire a desire to fix the harm caused by institutional racism.

Raikes Foundation

As many may already know, race can have a significant impact on an individual’s access to education. Our research shows that this limited access often means that the options available to racially diverse students are less than desirable in terms of quality of education. Poor education means reduced competitiveness in the job market and results in the perpetuation of a system of restricted opportunity and extreme difficulty in socioeconomic status improvement for minorities in our society.

The Raikes Foundation is an organization that strives to create an equitable education system where, not only race, but class, gender, language, and ability-status will affect a student’s chances of graduating from high school, being suspended, or completing a post-secondary degree.

Raikes Foundation mission: The Raikes Foundation invests in youth-serving institutions and systems to make them more effective in supporting all young people, especially those who have been most marginalized.

Check them out to learn more about their goals for equitable education environments and what you can do to aid in the achievement of these goals.

Personal experience of racial prejudices on work

As a biracial woman, Dr. Tammy Hodo has experienced two different Americas: one for whites and one for blacks. As a Ph.D. social scientist who studies the impacts the variables of race, class, ethnicity, and sex have on life chances, she has incredible insight into how to start the difficult dialogue about the irrelevance of enduring racial constructs. From this, we can have a wider view how race actually affects one’s employment status though they have outstanding performance that should be deserved to be hired.

Understanding black-white disparities in labor market outcomes requires models that account for persistent discrimination and unequal bargaining power

click to read in detail the article about how race impacts unemployment

This article discusses about The differences in race and socioeconomic status between black and white people do affect the rate of unemployment in the US labour market.

There are some notable evidences in this article that further investigate how unemployment was adjusted and affected by race. More specifcally, there is a 2-to-1 disparity in unemployment that happens between black and white workers. Though African American complete high school and college, they are always being treated unequal on employment. In 2019, the typical (median) black worker earned 24.4% less per hour than the typical white worker. Moreover, black workers are more likely to live in lower-wage Southern states leaves an unexplained gap of 14.9% in 2019 (out of a total average gap of 26.5%). Hence, the median wage of black workers grew at a meager 5.2% (0.1% per year) and the median wage of white workers grew 20.0% (0.5% per year). For black workers, there is also a long history of racially motivated exclusion, exploitation, and oppression that contributes to the assumed inferiority of black labor and the normalization of racial inequality.

Not only that but the article also highlight examples of how disparities in bargaining power play out for black workers and review the prevailing economic theories used to interpret racial inequalities in labor market outcomes and present challenges to and shortcomings of those theories.

In conclusion, interventions are required to address these inequalities. Policies geared toward maximizing employment and limiting the depth and duration of recessions are essential to establishing a new balance of power that makes workers less vulnerable to limited job prospects and low wages. Furthermore, labor unions play an important role in giving workers a stronger collective voice to advocate for higher pay, better benefits, training and promotional opportunities, and protections against discrimination and harassment.

White-Space_Black-Hood

The image by Walika is an adaptation of Sheryl Cashin’s book White Space, Black Hood. It gives a strong contrast between a normal and a disadvantaged socioeconomic status on the basis of race. It may not provide any evidence of belonging to a segregated neighborhood but speaks boldly about the issue mentioned before. The house on the left seems to be brand new; covered with a grey tiled roof and white paint while the one on the right looks dilapidated and old with creepers crawling over the walls and broken tiles all over the roof. The difference in color of the two openly represents two racial groups- the white (white house) and the black (reddish black house) community. The image reminds one of racial segregation and shows how deeply it is connected to various aspects of socioeconomic status when one dissects the cause for the condition of the house on the right. From an economic perspective one of the biggest causes of this difference could certainly be wealth gap as the house on the left looks more polished and expensive compared to the right. Moreover, this reaps the idea of racial pay gap as according to stlouisfed.org black families have had about $957,000 less wealth, on average, compared with white families in 2022. Lastly, with respect to social status the image suggests the idea of white superiority over the black community when one simply takes the difference in the appearance of the two houses into consideration once more- the white, polished on the left and the rusty, unkempt on the right.
